Tiny Project 4: Pacific Rhododendron

Learn all about Washington’s state flower

Rhododendron Macrophyllum

State Flower of Washington

Plant Name: Rhododendron Macrophyllum

Common Names: Pacific Rhododendron, Coast Rhododendron, California Rhododendron, California Rosebay

Photos

Size

Pacific rhododendrons can grow in many different sizes depending on where they are planted. On the smaller side, they might only reach about 3 to 5 feet tall. In the right conditions though, they can grow like a small tree, reaching up to 20 to 25 feet. They usually grow taller in shady places and stay shorter and bushier in sunnier spots.

Where It Grows

This plant is native to the Pacific Northwest. You can find it west of the Cascade Mountains, from southern British Columbia through Washington and Oregon down into northern California. It grows in forests, along roadsides, and even up in the mountains at elevations around 6,000 feet.

Best Climate and Growing Conditions

Pacific rhododendrons like cool, damp areas with acidic, well-draining soil. In the wild, they are often found under big trees such as Douglas fir, Western red cedar, Western hemlock, and Sitka spruce. They can survive in drier conditions, but they thrive when the soil holds some moisture. They also grow well in disturbed areas like roadsides, where they even help prevent erosion.

Fun Facts

Poisonous but interesting

Did you know rhododendrons are beautiful but a little dangerous? Every part of the plant has a natural toxin called grayanotoxin. If someone or some animal eats it, it can cause nausea, weakness, and even heart issues. Even bees that visit rhododendrons can make “mad honey,” which has been known to make people sick. Luckily, most of us just admire these flowers from a safe distance—and that’s exactly how they’re meant to be enjoyed.

Blooming season

These plants really know how to put on a show. They bloom in the spring and early summer, usually from April through July. The ones growing lower down in valleys and near the coast bloom first, while the mountain rhodies hold out until later. By late summer, August through September, their seed pods are ready to pop.

A state symbol with a story

The Pacific rhododendron wasn’t chosen as Washington’s state flower by accident. Back in 1892, local women were asked to pick a flower to represent Washington at the 1893 World’s Fair in Chicago. They voted for this vibrant, tough little plant that thrives in the Northwest. People loved the choice so much that decades later, in 1959, it was officially made the state flower. Today, hikers and gardeners still think of it as one of the most iconic plants in Washington.
